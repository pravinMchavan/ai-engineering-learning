Level of Measurement

Definition:
The level of measurement describes how data values are classified, ordered, and measured. It tells us what mathematical operations and statistical methods are appropriate for the data.

Explanation:
There are four standard levels of measurement: nominal, ordinal, interval, and ratio. Each level has increasingly stronger properties:
- Nominal: categories only, no natural order.
- Ordinal: categories with meaningful order, but intervals are not equal.
- Interval: ordered values with equal intervals, but no true zero.
- Ratio: ordered values with equal intervals and a true zero point.

Examples:
- Nominal: colors of cars (red, blue, green), blood type (A, B, AB, O)
- Ordinal: education level (high school, bachelor, master, doctorate), customer satisfaction (poor, fair, good, excellent)
- Interval: temperature in Celsius or Fahrenheit, calendar years
- Ratio: weight, height, income, distance

Types of Data

1) Qualitative Data (Categorical Data)
Definition:
Qualitative data describe qualities or characteristics and are expressed as categories rather than numbers.

Explanation:
Qualitative data represent labels or names that identify attributes of objects or people. This type of data cannot be meaningfully added or averaged, but it can be counted and classified.

Subtypes:
- Nominal Data: categories with no natural order
  * Example: eye color (brown, blue, green)
  * Example: type of car (sedan, SUV, truck)
- Ordinal Data: categories with a meaningful order
  * Example: clothing sizes (small, medium, large)
  * Example: survey ratings (strongly disagree, disagree, neutral, agree, strongly agree)

Examples:
- Gender (male, female, non-binary)
- Marital status (single, married, divorced)
- Product categories (electronics, furniture, clothing)

2) Quantitative Data (Numerical Data)
Definition:
Quantitative data represent measurable quantities and are expressed using numbers.

Explanation:
Quantitative data count or measure things and support arithmetic operations such as addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division. They can be used to calculate averages, differences, and other numerical summaries.

Subtypes:
- Interval Data: numeric values with equal intervals, but no true zero
  * Example: temperature in Celsius, where zero does not mean “no heat”
  * Example: year of birth
- Ratio Data: numeric values with equal intervals and a true zero
  * Example: weight, where zero means no weight
  * Example: height, distance, time, income

Examples:
- Number of students in a class
- Salary in rupees
- Age in years
- Test scores out of 100
